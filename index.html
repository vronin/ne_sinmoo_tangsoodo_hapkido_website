What Is Sin Moo Tang Soo Hapkido?

Sin Moo Tang Soo Hapkido is a complete martial art rooted in the Korean tradition of Hapkido — the art of coordinated power. Our practice blends powerful joint locks, throws, and dynamic kicks with meditation, breathing exercises, and a philosophy centered on personal growth.

Our group, North East Sin Moo Tang Soo Hapkido, is led by practitioners trained directly under Grandmaster John L. Godwin (10th Dan), who himself studied for decades under Hapkido's legendary founder, Dojunim Ji Han-jae. We carry forward an unbroken lineage from the source of the art.

The Name

Sin Moo  神武

"Higher mind martial art." The spiritual and philosophical dimension developed by Ji Han-jae — treating meditation and internal development as equal to physical technique.

Tang Soo

A nod to GM Godwin's deep background in Tang Soo Do, a traditional Korean striking art. GM Godwin holds an authorized Tang Soo Kwan — a certified school style blending Sin Moo Hapkido with Tang Soo Do.

Hapkido  合氣道

"The way of coordinated energy." Hap (harmony), Ki (energy), Do (the path). A complete self-defense art of joint locks, throws, kicks, and strikes.

The Story Behind the Art

Where Hapkido Began

Hapkido was shaped by one remarkable man: Ji Han-jae (1936–2026). Born in Andong, Korea, during the Japanese occupation, Ji trained under three very different teachers — and it was the combination of their teachings that created something entirely new.

  • Choi Yong-sul — joint locks, throws, and grappling rooted in Japanese Daito-Ryu Aiki-jujutsu: close-range, powerful, and highly technical
  • "Taoist Lee" — Korean kicking traditions from Taekkyon, weapons (staff, short stick, cane, sword), and Taoist breathing exercises
  • "Grandma" (Halmeoni) — deep meditation, spiritual development, and Sam-Il-Sin-Do: the unity of body, mind, and spirit

In 1957, Ji opened his famous Sung Moo Kwan dojang in Seoul and named the art Hapkido. He went on to serve as head instructor for the Korean Presidential Security Forces and famously appeared alongside Bruce Lee in the 1972 film Game of Death — after Bruce Lee sought him out specifically to learn Hapkido's spinning kicks.

The Birth of Sin Moo Hapkido

After political upheaval in Korea, Ji spent a year in prison around 1980. During that time, he turned inward — practicing the meditation he'd learned from Grandma and Taoist Lee, restructuring the kicking curriculum, and developing the spiritual framework he'd been refining for decades. What emerged was Sin Moo Hapkido — "higher mind martial art" — formally established in 1983.

Sin Moo Hapkido is not a different fighting style. It is the complete version of what Ji Han-jae intended Hapkido to be: physical technique elevated by mental clarity and spiritual awareness. While other Hapkido lineages focus primarily on physical fighting, Sin Moo Hapkido treats meditation, breathing, and philosophical development as equally essential.

Ji moved permanently to the United States in 1984 and passed away on January 28, 2026, at age 89. Approximately 60% of all Hapkido schools worldwide trace their lineage back to him.

Core Philosophy

Yu Won Hwa: Water, Circle, Harmony

Every technique in Sin Moo Hapkido is built on three principles. These are not abstract ideals — they govern every joint lock, every throw, every footwork decision.

💧

Yu — Water

Be like water. Don't oppose force directly — flow around it, redirect it, and let the attacker's energy work against them. Water is soft to touch, yet it erodes boulders over time.

◯

Won — Circle

Move in circles, not straight lines. When someone attacks in a straight line, redirect that force in a circle, adding their momentum to your own. Circles can be large (a full-body throw) or small (a wrist lock).

☯

Hwa — Harmony

Stay relaxed. Never fight strength against strength. If pushed, move with the push. If pulled, add to the pull. Remain calm while the attacker exhausts themselves against your non-resistance.

Practical Concepts

Pull‑Push

When they pull, help them pull. When they push, pull. Never oppose the direction of force — use it. The attacker's energy becomes your technique.

Reaction

A gentle grab gets a gentle redirect. A powerful attack gets a powerful response. The attacker chooses the intensity of their own consequence.

Circular Motion

Straight-line attacks are redirected into circular paths. This is how a smaller person controls a larger one — by turning linear force into rotational energy.

The 9 Rules

Ji Han-jae taught nine rules for living, drawn from Confucian, Buddhist, and Taoist traditions:

Body — Confucian

Eat well. Maintain healthy habits. Balance activity with rest.

Mind — Buddhist

Don't let anger control you. Don't dwell in sadness. Don't be consumed by greed.

Spirit — Taoist

Seek balance with water (fluidity), air (breath and energy), and sunshine (warmth and openness).

What to Expect in Training

Sin Moo Hapkido is a practical, self-defense-oriented martial art. There are no forms or patterns to memorize — every minute of training is spent on real techniques applied with a partner. A typical class includes:

  • Danjeon breathing — deep abdominal breathing to center the mind and develop internal energy (Ki)
  • Warm-up and stretching — functional conditioning for the techniques ahead
  • Technique practice — joint locks, throws, strikes, kicks, and defenses practiced with a partner
  • Break falls — learning to land safely is one of the first and most important skills
  • Meditation — classes close with seated meditation

You'll start with foundational wrist escapes, the first 10 kicks, and basic choke defenses. As you progress, you'll add clothing grab defenses, cross-hand techniques, weapons, and increasingly sophisticated applications of the same core principles. The curriculum is designed so that each belt level builds directly on the last — nothing is wasted, and everything connects.
